# Impact-Aware Manipulation and Force Optimization

Recent research has shown that robots can intentionally use **high-speed contacts** if the controller is aware of impact dynamics. For example, Wang et al. (2023) formulate a task-space QP controller that **“regulates contact velocity in a modified search space”** to ensure post-impact states (impulse, force, momentum, etc.) remain within safe bounds. They add convex constraints (polyhedral sets) on impulses, contact forces, center-of-mass velocity and angular momentum to make impacts “hardware-affordable”. Their experiments (Figure 4 below) show that by limiting approach speed the QP ensures rapid grasp motions do not exceed robot limits.

This lets the robot grasp boxes with high speed while keeping impact safely within hardware limits.*  

In parallel, **QP-based multi-task control** has long supported force tasks. Bouyarmane and Kheddar (2019) show that a whole-body QP controller can **compute and “suggest” optimal contact wrenches** subject to constraints, and then servo the robot to realize those forces. Concretely, their controller can either track a user-specified desired contact force or use its internally computed optimal force, closing the loop on that wrench. Similarly, Nava et al. (2020) demonstrate a QP controller for an aerial manipulator that integrates position and force objectives: the QP solves a multi-task optimization (end-effector pose, interaction force, etc.) under constraints, yielding stable contact behaviors. Explicit force-feedback (e.g. from a wrist force sensor) further improves tracking precision in these controllers. In short, modern QP frameworks (like [mc_rtc](https://github.com/stack-of-tasks/mc_rtc) or Stack-of-Tasks) can handle hybrid tasks, including contact force regulation, by treating force goals as tasks or constraints in the solver.

Separately, there is rich literature on **grasp force optimization** (usually static or offline). For multi-arm or dexterous hands, one typically solves a convex optimization to **distribute forces under friction and joint limits**. For instance, Zhou et al. (2022) formulate dual-arm grasp force optimization as a convex problem, choosing criteria like “minimization of contact forces to prevent damage”. Jia et al. (referenced in Yu et al. 2025) likewise optimize fingertip forces to satisfy friction-cone and equilibrium constraints with minimum norm objectives. In an agricultural context, Yu et al. (2025) use a gradient-based method to compute a *minimum stable grasp force* for apple picking under friction/balance constraints. These works show that optimized contact forces can improve grip safety and efficiency – but they typically **compute force references offline or in a separate loop**, not directly embedded in a real-time impact controller.

## Research Gaps and Novelty

From the above, we see a gap: **no prior work fully integrates on-line grasp-force optimization into an impact-aware QP controller.** Existing impact-aware QP controllers focus on limiting contact speed and ensuring safe state jumps, but they do not adapt the target contact force based on an optimization criterion. Conversely, grasp-force optimization studies compute ideal force distributions, but **treat contact forces as fixed references**, without coupling to fast impact transitions. In other words, current controllers either (*a*) adjust kinematics for impact resilience or (*b*) compute static optimal forces for grasping, but rarely do both together. 

A publishable contribution could exploit this gap by developing a **unified QP framework** that dynamically computes *on-demand* contact forces at grasping, while still being impact-aware. For example, one could add a secondary objective in the QP to minimize the norm of the contact wrench (or maximize stability margin) subject to tasks, effectively making the controller choose “just enough” force to secure the object. This extends Bouyarmane’s idea of QP force tasks by making the desired wrench outcome part of the optimization. Likewise, the system could integrate classical grasp-optimization constraints (friction cones, grasp stability) directly into the QP constraints or cost, blending them with the impact-state polyhedra of Wang et al. If successful, the result would be a robot that not only grabs objects quickly and safely (impact-aware) but also uses **minimally necessary forces** to do so (force-optimal). 

Key points to highlight as novel contributions might include:

- **On-line force adaptation:** Unlike prior work, the controller computes optimal contact forces in real-time, based on current configuration and task. For instance, it might solve a small convex problem each control step to update the force reference, embedding it as a QP task. 
- **Hybrid impact-force QP formulation:** The method seamlessly combines impact-state constraints (from frictional impact models) with force-optimization objectives. This requires careful design to keep the QP convex and solvable at high rate. 
- **Robust high-speed grasping:** Demonstrate that the robot can execute fast grasp maneuvers (like in Wang 2023 and van Steen 2023) **while using optimized low forces**, reducing slippage or deformation risk. The pitch: “**Impact-aware grasping with minimal force**.” 
- **Physical validation:** Implement on a real robot (e.g. using mc_rtc or HRP/Panda) to show improved performance. Metrics could include maximum impact forces, stability of grasp, or energy usage (force-norm), comparing to baseline controllers.

## Publishable? Yes – with Clear Focus

Given the novelty of integrating these elements, the work **could be publishable** if the contribution is clearly distinguished. The literature shows that (1) *impact-tolerant QP control* is a hot topic, and (2) *force distribution optimization* is well-studied but usually separate. A paper that **bridges these** by incorporating real-time force optimization into an impact-aware QP is a logical next step. In the write-up/pitch, emphasize how your approach “closes the loop” on force choice under impact – for example, by citing Bouyarmane’s idea of the QP planning its own force reference and then adding that your scheme makes that force *optimal on-demand*. Mention that this leads to safer high-speed grasps: e.g. your experiments might mirror Wang et al.’s swift box grabs but with lower peak forces and post-impact corrections. 

In summary, frame the paper as **“impact-aware manipulation with online force optimization.”** Highlight that this addresses a current gap: robots often either avoid impacts or use fixed force commands, whereas you enable agile, impact-rich tasks *with intelligent force control*. With a solid evaluation and clear performance gains, this could be pitched to a robotics conference or journal (e.g. ICRA/IROS or IEEE RA-L) as a meaningful extension of task-space QP controllers.
