# Key Objectives and Hypotheses  
The goal of this paper is to **show that adaptively allocating dual-arm squeeze (normal) forces based on the object’s motion can significantly improve efficiency and safety** compared to a fixed safety margin.  In other words, we want to prove that coupling the minimal grasp force to the motion state (a “task-on-demand” safety margin) will reduce wasted actuation effort (and thus energy/power) without compromising slip stability.  We also aim to demonstrate that **biasing the force allocation toward the arm with more joint torque capacity** further improves overall performance.  Concretely:  

- **Motion-adaptive grip force reduces effort:**  We hypothesize that tying the minimum grasp force to object velocity (instead of a constant margin) will *lower the average commanded squeeze force* and the total actuator work.  This is directly inspired by human grip behavior: experiments show people use just enough grip force to prevent slip, balancing minimal effort with a small safety margin.  Likewise, adaptive grasp designs can hold objects securely with much lower forces – one study found an adaptive prosthetic hand could support daily tasks with under 10 N of grip force.  By analogy, our motion-driven scheme should yield noticeably lower total squeeze force (hence lower power draw) than a constant-margin controller, especially when the object is at rest.  

- **Slip safety is maintained when needed:**  Even though we reduce unnecessary force at rest, we expect the *dynamic slip margin* to remain safe during motion.  By scheduling a larger safety margin only while the object moves (velocity-based), the robot will still apply enough grip when accelerations and inertial loads are high, then relax when static.  We will verify that the minimum tangential‐force‐to‐friction ratio stays well below 1 under all conditions, indicating no slip.  

- **Balanced load and torque awareness:**  Our QP also enforces balanced force distribution (penalizing one arm doing all the work) and can include a joint-torque cost.  We hypothesize that adding the torque-aware quadratic terms will shift squeeze effort onto the arm with more actuation headroom.  This should *increase each arm’s torque margin* (distance to its limits) relative to a force-unaware allocation.  (For example, [24] introduces a “joint-torque margin” metric measuring how close each joint is to its limits; our approach aims to maximize that margin by design.)  

- **Concrete contributions:**  In summary, we are *proving that* (1) a two-variable QP can allocate normal forces to both arms in real time while enforcing friction and actuation bounds, (2) making the safety floor velocity-dependent (task-on-demand) measurably reduces overall effort, and (3) adding a small torque-penalty reshapes the force split to protect the arm with tighter joint capacity.  We will contrast each element against simpler baselines (e.g. fixed safety margin, no torque cost) and show the improvements in clear metrics.  

## Experimental Validation Plan and Metrics  
To test these hypotheses, we will run coordinated pick–move–place trials on a dual xArm7 setup.  We will compare several conditions: (a) constant safety margin (baseline), (b) scheduled (planned) motion-adaptive demand, (c) velocity-estimated motion-adaptive demand, and (d) a hybrid feedforward+feedback demand.  We will also test the torque-aware extension by running (e) with equal torque weights vs (f) asymmetric weights (favoring one arm).  

We will record the following metrics in each trial:  

- **Internal (grasp) force tracking:** Measure how well the QP achieves the commanded internal (squeeze) force.  We expect all variants to track the target, but particularly check for overshoot or steady-state error.  
- **Slip-margin utilization:** Compute the ratio of tangential contact force to friction limit (per arm, from force sensors).  We expect this ratio to stay safely below 1.0 at all times.  A successful adaptive scheme should *not* see higher slip-risk than the baseline; ideally it uses just enough normal force to keep this ratio comfortably low during motion, but much lower when static.  
- **Total squeeze effort:** Quantify the cumulative actuation effort as \(E = \int \|x(t)\|\;dt\) (where \(x=[F_{n,L},F_{n,R}]\)).  This proxy for energy or motor work should be **significantly less** for the motion-adaptive methods than for the constant-margin baseline.  (By hypothesis, tying force to motion means less wasted holding force when the object isn’t moving.)  
- **Joint torques and margins:** Record each arm’s joint torques over time and their proximity to limits.  We expect that **with the torque-aware cost**, the maximum torque (or joint-torque “margin” as in [24]) will be lower on the arm that was previously at risk of saturation.  In other words, the peak and RMS torques should be more balanced between arms, and the minimum distance to any joint limit should increase under torque-weighted allocation.  
- **Computation time:** Finally, we will profile the QP solve times to ensure real-time feasibility (the QP has only 2 variables, so it should be very fast).  

Overall, the experiment will show clear comparisons of (i) total grip effort and energy usage, (ii) slip-margin safety, and (iii) joint-torque distribution, between the proposed adaptive/torque-aware strategy and simpler baselines.  

## Expected Results and Contributions  
Based on the above, we expect to “prove” the following points to the reviewer:  

- **Efficiency gain:** The adaptive schemes should use *noticeably less total squeezing force* than a constant-margin controller, especially during stationary or low-motion phases.  This implies less actuator current and power consumed.  For instance, if the baseline always holds an extra 10–20 N for safety at rest, our method will drop to just the static minimum at rest, trimming off that wasted force.  We anticipate at least a 10–30% reduction in integrated normal force, which translates to real power savings (as seen in prior work on adaptive grasping).  

- **Maintained safety:** Despite the lower forces, the slip margin (tangential force/friction) should never exceed the baseline’s maximum.  In fact, by proactively increasing force *during* motion, we should avoid any incipient slip episodes (slip margin stays below 1).  We will show plots of friction bound utilization over time for each method to illustrate this.  

- **Torque balance:** Introducing the torque-cost terms should clearly shift load to the “stronger” arm.  For example, if we induce an asymmetric condition (one arm near a singular posture), we expect the torque-aware QP to reduce that arm’s peak torque compared to the naive QP.  The result will be a higher minimum joint-torque margin and lower peak torque on the strained arm, consistent with the actuation-aware planning literature.  

- **Trade-offs and parameter effects:** We will also sweep the cost weights \(\alpha,\beta,\gamma_L,\gamma_R\) to illustrate their roles.  Small \(\alpha\) (grasp cost) emphasizes minimal force, large \(\beta\) enforces balance, and nonzero \(\gamma\) protects torque.  This will give intuition for tuning and show robustness (e.g. the solution continuously interpolates between goals without sudden jumps).  

In summary, the paper’s point is that **adaptively modulating and optimally allocating grasp forces yields concrete benefits in a dual-arm task**.  By comparing our QP‐based method to fixed-margin baselines, we will *prove* that it lowers actuation effort and energy use while retaining safety and arm stability.  This is analogous to how humans and advanced grippers automatically minimize grip force for efficiency, and we expect our robotic implementation to demonstrate the same principle in real hardware.  These results, along with a discussion of limitations (e.g. the linearized friction constraint and static weight choices), will make the paper’s contributions and goals absolutely clear to reviewers.
